Decision-Making & Belief Biases

06

How proposals get judged, prices get anchored, and "obvious" calls get made — usually after the fact.

01 / DECISION & BELIEF

Belief Bias

When logic loses to likability.

Flip for the breakdown
Belief Bias
Why it matters

We judge an argument's validity by how much we like the conclusion or the person making it, not by the actual logic. A buyer may approve a weak proposal because they like the rep, or reject a strong one because the tone felt off.

Real-world example

A prospect dismisses a well-reasoned AI migration case because the email tone felt "salesy," not because the ROI logic was flawed.

Notice the trap

Am I judging this idea on its merits, or on how much I like who's presenting it?

Break the pattern

Strip the argument from its source and re-evaluate it on logic alone.

Would I still find this convincing if a stranger said it?

02 / DECISION & BELIEF

Confirmation Bias

We seek what supports us — and screen out what challenges us.

Flip for the breakdown
Confirmation Bias
Why it matters

Once a hypothesis exists ("this content format works"), teams unconsciously favor data that confirms it and dismiss data that doesn't.

Real-world example

A newsletter strategy is deemed a success by citing the one segment with high open rates, ignoring three segments that underperformed.

Notice the trap

Am I only looking at data that proves I'm right?

Break the pattern

Actively search for the disconfirming case before drawing a conclusion.

What evidence would change my mind, and have I looked for it?

03 / DECISION & BELIEF

Sunk Cost Fallacy

You can't get it back — but it still controls you.

Flip for the breakdown
Sunk Cost Fallacy
Why it matters

Past investment — time, budget, a tool already built — keeps pulling more resources in, even when the data says stop. Common in long content series or campaigns that aren't converting.

Real-world example

A team keeps refining a newsletter format that isn't growing subscribers, because "we already spent three months on it."

Notice the trap

Am I continuing because it's working, or because I've already invested in it?

Break the pattern

Evaluate the decision only on future costs and benefits, ignoring what's already spent.

If I were starting fresh today, would I still choose this?

04 / DECISION & BELIEF

Decoy Effect

A third, inferior option nudges us toward the "reasonable" one.

Flip for the breakdown
Decoy Effect
Why it matters

Our preference between two choices shifts when a clearly inferior third option is introduced. Heavily exploited in pricing and bundling — but can backfire if used too transparently with sophisticated B2B buyers.

Real-world example

A pricing page adds a deliberately weak "Standard" tier so the "Pro" tier looks like the obvious smart choice.

Notice the trap

Am I reacting to a real value comparison, or to an added option that's just there to shift my choice?

Break the pattern

Remove the decoy mentally and re-evaluate the remaining options on their own.

Would I still choose this if the third option didn't exist?

05 / DECISION & BELIEF

Anchoring Bias

The first number we see becomes the reference point for everything after.

Flip for the breakdown
Anchoring Bias
Why it matters

Strongly shapes how prospects judge pricing, ROI claims, and proposals — whoever sets the first number controls the frame.

Real-world example

A sales deck leads with "$2M in losses from manual underwriting" before presenting the solution price, making the fee feel small by comparison.

Notice the trap

Is my judgment of "expensive" or "cheap" based on a number I saw earlier, even if it's irrelevant?

Break the pattern

Evaluate the offer on its own merits, independent of the first number mentioned.

What number did I see first, and is it actually relevant to this decision?

06 / DECISION & BELIEF

Framing Effect

The same fact, worded differently, changes how we judge it.

Flip for the breakdown
Framing Effect
Why it matters

"95% success rate" vs "5% failure rate" shapes buyer reaction even though the information is identical. Affects pricing pages, case study headlines, and proposal language.

Real-world example

"Cut underwriting time by 80%" lands very differently than "Underwriting still takes 20% of the original time," even though they say the same thing.

Notice the trap

Am I reacting to the substance of this claim, or to how it's been worded?

Break the pattern

Restate the same fact in the opposite frame and see if your reaction changes.

Would I feel the same way if this were phrased the other way around?

Emotional & Motivational Biases

04

Where gut feeling, ownership, and urgency override the spreadsheet.

07 / EMOTIONAL & MOTIVATIONAL

Affect Heuristic

When feelings steer the facts.

Flip for the breakdown
Affect Heuristic
Why it matters

Quick emotional reactions — "this feels risky," "this feels exciting" — substitute for actual risk/benefit analysis. Shows up in pricing decisions, vendor selection, and campaign approval.

Real-world example

A newsletter subject line that "feels exciting" gets greenlit over one that tested better, because gut reaction overrode the data.

Notice the trap

Am I reacting to how this makes me feel, or to what it actually does?

Break the pattern

Separate the emotional reaction from the data; review the numbers without the framing.

If I removed the emotional packaging, what would the facts alone say?

08 / EMOTIONAL & MOTIVATIONAL

Endowment Effect

Once it's mine, it's worth more.

Flip for the breakdown
Endowment Effect
Why it matters

We place higher value on things we own simply because we own them. Leads to overvaluing internal work — a logo, a deck template, a pitch — and resisting outside feedback.

Real-world example

A founder won't update an old pitch deck structure because "we built this," even though prospects find it confusing.

Notice the trap

Am I valuing this because it's good, or because it's mine/ours?

Break the pattern

Get a neutral outsider's evaluation, blind to who made it.

What do I overvalue just because I created or own it?

09 / EMOTIONAL & MOTIVATIONAL

Scarcity Bias

We value things more when they seem limited or about to disappear.

Flip for the breakdown
Scarcity Bias
Why it matters

Used in event marketing ("limited seats for the mixer") and offer deadlines — effective in moderation, but erodes trust if the scarcity is fake.

Real-world example

"Only 10 spots left" on an event signup page drives faster RSVPs — but repeating it for every event teaches prospects to ignore it.

Notice the trap

Is this scarcity real, or a manufactured sense of urgency?

Break the pattern

Ask whether the deadline or limit would actually be enforced if you waited.

What happens if I don't act before the deadline — is the scarcity actually real?

10 / EMOTIONAL & MOTIVATIONAL

Loss Aversion

Losing something looms larger than gaining the equivalent.

Flip for the breakdown
Loss Aversion
Why it matters

People act faster to avoid a loss than to capture an equal gain — shapes how offers, renewal reminders, and risk-focused pitches are framed.

Real-world example

"Don't let manual processes keep costing you $50K/month" converts better than "Save $50K/month with automation," even though the math is identical.

Notice the trap

Am I reacting more strongly because this is framed as a loss rather than a gain?

Break the pattern

Reframe the message as a gain and see if the underlying case still holds up.

Would this still feel urgent if it were framed as an opportunity instead of a loss?

Memory & Recall Biases

02

How postmortems quietly rewrite themselves once the outcome is known.

11 / MEMORY & RECALL

Hindsight Bias

"I knew it all along."

Flip for the breakdown
Hindsight Bias
Why it matters

After a campaign succeeds or fails, we retroactively feel it was predictable, which erodes honest postmortems and inflates confidence in future forecasts.

Real-world example

After a webinar underperforms, the team insists "we knew that headline wouldn't land," even though no one flagged it beforehand.

Notice the trap

Did I actually predict this, or am I rewriting my memory now that I know the outcome?

Break the pattern

Check what was written down beforehand — briefs, predictions — before declaring it was "obvious."

What did I write down before I knew the result?

12 / MEMORY & RECALL

Illusory Truth Effect

Repetition makes a claim feel true, regardless of accuracy.

Flip for the breakdown
Illusory Truth Effect
Why it matters

Repeating a tagline, stat, or positioning claim enough times — internally or externally — can make a team believe it's well-established fact, even if it was never actually verified.

Real-world example

"AI will replace 40% of underwriting tasks" gets repeated across decks so often that no one remembers — or checks — where the number originally came from.

Notice the trap

Do I believe this because it's true, or because I've heard it repeated so often?

Break the pattern

Trace the claim back to its original source before repeating it again.

Where did this claim originally come from, and have I verified it myself?

Attention & Perception Biases

07

What the brain chooses to notice, and how it quietly rewrites what's actually there.

13 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Clustering Illusion

We see patterns in noise.

Flip for the breakdown
Clustering Illusion
Why it matters

Small samples — a few good leads from one channel, a couple of viral posts — get read as meaningful trends, leading to over-investment in what's actually randomness.

Real-world example

Two LinkedIn posts go viral in a week, so the team concludes "LinkedIn is our channel now" and shifts budget before enough data exists.

Notice the trap

Is this a real trend, or a small cluster I'm over-reading?

Break the pattern

Check the sample size and look at a longer time window before acting.

Would this pattern hold over 10x more data?

14 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Threat / Affective Bias

Attention locks onto danger and mood-matching signals first.

Flip for the breakdown
Threat / Affective Bias
Why it matters

The brain automatically prioritizes anything that reads as risky or emotionally charged over neutral information. In a pipeline review, one alarming churn data point can dominate the room even when the rest of the dashboard is healthy.

Real-world example

A single angry comment on a sponsored post pulls the team's full attention away from a campaign that's otherwise converting well above target.

Notice the trap

Am I fixated on this because it's the biggest risk, or because it's the loudest one?

Break the pattern

Deliberately scan the full picture before reacting to the one alarming signal.

If this hadn't felt threatening, would I still think it's the most important thing here?

15 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Inattentional Blindness

We miss what's fully visible when our focus is elsewhere.

Flip for the breakdown
Inattentional Blindness
Why it matters

Total focus on one task — like proofreading copy for tone — means an obvious error sitting in plain sight, like a broken link or wrong CTA, goes completely unnoticed.

Real-world example

A team spends an hour perfecting a landing page's headline and never notices the signup form button is pointing to the wrong URL.

Notice the trap

Have I been so focused on one element that I haven't actually looked at the whole thing?

Break the pattern

Hand the final review to someone who hasn't been staring at the same task.

Who's looking at this with fresh eyes before it ships?

16 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Change Blindness

We fail to notice gradual changes happening right in front of us.

Flip for the breakdown
Change Blindness
Why it matters

Slow, incremental drift — in brand voice, lead quality, or a metric's baseline — passes unnoticed because no single moment looks different from the one before it.

Real-world example

A newsletter's tone slowly drifts away from brand guidelines over six months of small edits, and no one flags it because each issue looked fine next to the last one.

Notice the trap

Am I only comparing this to last time, instead of to where we started?

Break the pattern

Periodically compare current output directly against the original baseline, not just the most recent version.

If I placed this next to what we made a year ago, would I still call it the same thing?

17 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Perceptual Set

We see what our experience primes us to expect.

Flip for the breakdown
Perceptual Set
Why it matters

Past experience, culture, and current mood shape what we expect to see — so two people can read the same client email and walk away with completely different impressions of its tone.

Real-world example

A marketer who's been burned by a slow-paying client reads neutral phrasing in a new prospect's email as a stalling tactic, while a colleague reads it as simply busy.

Notice the trap

Am I reading this based on what's actually written, or based on what past experience primed me to expect?

Break the pattern

Ask a colleague with no prior history on the account to read the same message and compare notes.

Would I interpret this the same way if I had no history with this person or topic?

18 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Negativity Bias

Bad feedback weighs more heavily than good feedback of equal size.

Flip for the breakdown
Negativity Bias
Why it matters

One critical comment can outweigh ten pieces of positive feedback in how a campaign, deck, or piece of content gets remembered and judged internally.

Real-world example

A newsletter gets 40 positive replies and one unsubscribe with a complaint — and the team meeting ends up entirely about the complaint.

Notice the trap

Is this one piece of negative feedback getting more airtime than its actual share of the response?

Break the pattern

Weigh feedback by volume and pattern, not by how loudly or recently it was said.

Out of all the feedback received, how representative is the negative piece I'm focused on?

19 / ATTENTION & PERCEPTION

Naïve Realism

The belief that we see the world exactly as it objectively is.

Flip for the breakdown
Naïve Realism
Why it matters

Assuming your read on a campaign, a client relationship, or a market is simply "the facts" makes disagreement look like the other person being unreasonable, rather than just having a different — equally partial — view.

Real-world example

Two team members disagree on why a campaign underperformed, each fully convinced their explanation is just "what happened," rather than one interpretation among several.

Notice the trap

Am I treating my read of this situation as the objective truth, rather than as one perspective?

Break the pattern

Actively ask what a reasonable person with different information might conclude instead.

What would someone who disagrees with me say they're seeing that I'm not?

Social & Attribution Biases

05

How rooms agree too quickly, and how confidence gets mistaken for competence.

20 / SOCIAL & ATTRIBUTION

Groupthink

Agreement feels good. Until it doesn't.

Flip for the breakdown
Groupthink
Why it matters

Teams converge on a decision to preserve harmony, suppressing dissent — risky in campaign approvals, pricing calls, or event planning where one dissenting voice could've caught a flaw.

Real-world example

Everyone nods along to a tagline for a launch event because no one wants to be the one who derails a meeting that's running long.

Notice the trap

Is everyone agreeing because it's right, or because no one wants to be the dissenter?

Break the pattern

Explicitly assign someone to play devil's advocate before finalizing.

If I disagreed right now, would I feel safe saying so?

21 / SOCIAL & ATTRIBUTION

Dunning–Kruger Effect

The less we know, the more certain we sound.

Flip for the breakdown
Dunning–Kruger Effect
Why it matters

People with shallow knowledge of a topic often pitch with more confidence than experts, which can mislead stakeholders or clients into trusting the wrong voice.

Real-world example

A junior team member confidently proposes an AI underwriting workflow in a client deck, missing nuances a domain expert would flag immediately.

Notice the trap

Am I, or is the person I'm listening to, confident because of expertise — or because of inexperience?

Break the pattern

Bring in a subject-matter expert to pressure-test confident claims before they go external.

Does this confidence come from depth, or from not seeing the complexity yet?

22 / SOCIAL & ATTRIBUTION

Social Proof Bias

We assume something is good because others are doing it.

Flip for the breakdown
Social Proof Bias
Why it matters

Heavily used — and overused — in B2B marketing via logos, testimonials, "X companies trust us." Effective until it feels generic or unverifiable.

Real-world example

A landing page lists 20 client logos but a savvy buyer wonders how many are minor or inactive accounts.

Notice the trap

Am I trusting this because of real evidence, or just because "everyone else is"?

Break the pattern

Ask for specifics — which client, what result, verifiable how?

If the social proof were removed, would the claim still hold up on its own?

23 / SOCIAL & ATTRIBUTION

Halo Effect

One good trait makes us assume everything else is good too.

Flip for the breakdown
Halo Effect
Why it matters

A slick rebrand or one marquee client name can make buyers assume technical quality without it being demonstrated.

Real-world example

A prospect assumes a vendor's AI capability is strong because their website design is excellent — two unrelated qualities.

Notice the trap

Am I generalizing from one impressive trait to the whole offering?

Break the pattern

Evaluate the specific claim in question on its own evidence.

Is this one good impression bleeding into my judgment of something unrelated?

24 / SOCIAL & ATTRIBUTION

Authority Bias

A claim carries more weight simply because of who said it.

Flip for the breakdown
Authority Bias
Why it matters

A quote from a "Chief AI Officer" carries more persuasive weight than the same insight from an unnamed analyst. Useful for content credibility, but can mean weak ideas get rubber-stamped because a senior person said them.

Real-world example

A vague AI roadmap gets approved largely because it was presented by a VP, while a more rigorous junior analyst's version was challenged line by line.

Notice the trap

Am I trusting this because of the evidence, or because of who said it?

Break the pattern

Evaluate the claim as if it came from an anonymous source.

Would I find this equally convincing if I didn't know who said it?

Probability & Statistical Biases

02

Where success stories and recent data quietly stand in for the whole picture.

25 / PROBABILITY & STATISTICAL

Survivorship Bias

We learn from winners — and forget the rest.

Flip for the breakdown
Survivorship Bias
Why it matters

Case studies and "what worked" retrospectives only feature the campaigns or clients that succeeded, hiding the much larger set that failed using the same approach.

Real-world example

A case study touts an AI pilot's success without mentioning the other clients where the same pilot stalled.

Notice the trap

Am I only seeing the success stories, and not the failures that used the same method?

Break the pattern

Actively ask "what's the failure rate of this approach?" before generalizing.

What does the full sample look like, not just the winners I'm hearing about?

26 / PROBABILITY & STATISTICAL

Recency Bias

We overweight the most recent information.

Flip for the breakdown
Recency Bias
Why it matters

A single good or bad client call, the last campaign's results, or this week's lead numbers can dominate strategic decisions more than they should — especially in fast-moving pipeline reviews.

Real-world example

One bad month of lead quality triggers a full strategy pivot, even though the prior six months showed a stable, healthy trend.

Notice the trap

Am I weighting this because it's most important, or just because it happened most recently?

Break the pattern

Look at the full trend line, not just the latest data point.

Would I draw the same conclusion if I only had last quarter's data and not this week's?

Bandwagon Effect

BONUS

Adopting a belief mainly because it's gaining popularity — not because you evaluated it yourself.

27 / STANDALONE

Bandwagon Effect

We adopt a belief or behavior mainly because it's gaining popularity, not because we evaluated it ourselves.

Flip for the breakdown
Bandwagon Effect
Why it matters

Drives FOMO-based adoption of trends ("everyone's doing AI agents now") — useful for urgency in marketing, risky for your own strategic decisions if you're following the herd instead of your data.

Real-world example

A company rushes to add "AI-powered" to every product description because competitors are doing it, without a clear case for what it adds.

Notice the trap

Am I doing this because it's right for us, or because everyone else seems to be doing it?

Break the pattern

Ask what specific evidence — not popularity — supports this choice for our situation.

If no one else were doing this, would I still think it's a good idea?
